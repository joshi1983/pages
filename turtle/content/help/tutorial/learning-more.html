You've learned a lot. You've learned enough to make drawings and animations in WebLogo. We'll give tips on how to master WebLogo.

Get Ideas

Ideas for drawings and animation can be found in the examples you can search with File -> Load Example. Search with the keyword "simple" to see examples that are ideal for beginners.

Countless other sources of inspiration can be found on pinterest. Some good sites for public domain images include publicdomainvectors.org, or svgshare.com.

Create

Turn ideas from imagination into code.

Start with drawings that require small amounts of code. Some short examples can be found with the keyword "simple" in WebLogo's examples.

After creating a few simple drawings, challenge yourself to more sophisticated drawings with more lines of code or more difficult math.

Share

If Deriving From Other People's Work

Sharing is great but be careful if your drawing is derived from copyrighted work. If your work is designed to look similar to someone else's image or design, review the copyright policy of the other person's work before publicly sharing your version. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=APOBZzhDIPA has helpful discussion about copyright issues on derivative artwork.

A lot of the examples in WebLogo involve national flags, images from wikipedia because they tend to be public domain or very unrestricted. Any WebLogo examples are provided to learn from but a lot are inspired by third party source images. You can find the sources of inspiration mentioned in code comments. Before sharing any work of yours derived from WebLogo's examples, verify that copyright policies of the source images let you do that. WebLogo's developers don't want to restrict use of the examples but how you use them can be further restricted by their source images.

You can learn more about finding free or public domain images by watching https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ReHGSGwV4-A. You can also see Navigating Open Licensing to learn about different types of standard licenses.

Share your creations with friends, family, and others. Sharing what you make, learn, and your ideas can help you enjoy learning. Getting constructive feedback from others can motivate you to continue challenging yourself. You can also help others learn what you have.

Helping others break through difficult problems can also boost your confidence and polish your skills.

You can share images by uploading them to social media.

  • facebook and instagram can be good for sharing with friends and family. facebook groups like stringart training and sharing can be a nice place to share string art designs with string artists.
  • pinterest can be good for inspiring others.
  • twitter can get your drawing seen by lots of people.
  • reddit can be good for sparking discussion about your drawing.

Drawings that you want others to freely copy and use can be shared at archive.org, publicdomainvectors.org, or svgshare.com.

If a drawing can help illustrate an article on wikipedia, you can upload and add the image to the article. Other volunteers will review your edits and they'll become permanent if others find your contribution helpful.

You can share animations by uploading and publishing them on youtube.
